What's actually integer (W4A8)
mac_blocks_wv (used for both X1 and X2 phases):

Inner hot loop (line 200–201): INT8 × INT4 × UINT6 → INT32 — pure integer MACs
8 partial int32_t accumulators per block — no FP adder latency constraints
FP32 only appears once per block at line 216: d[b] * (x_scale * (float)sw) — a single multiply to apply the fp16 per-block scale
Why FP32 can't be avoided there
Q4_K stores two fp16 values per 256-element block: d (scale) and dmin (minimum-subtraction scale). The final result for each block is:


result = d × (x_scale × Σ(xi × qi × sci)) − dmin × (x_scale × Σ(xi × mni))
The d and dmin are fp16 with arbitrary magnitude — you can't represent them faithfully in a fixed integer range. So one FP32 multiply per block (not per element) is unavoidable.

Neural networks are trained in FP32/BF16, then the weights are quantized for storage. The activations (the tensors flowing between layers) remain FP32 throughout. You can confirm this in two ways:

In lfm2.cpp:


cur = ggml_add(ctx0, cur, ffn_out);   // line 201 — add expects F32
And in the ggml.c builder you already have:


result = ggml_new_tensor(ctx, GGML_TYPE_F32, 4, ne);
The next layer's build_norm (RMSnorm) and the residual add both consume FP32. The rule in llama.cpp is: weight tensors are quantized, activation tensors are always FP32. The GGUF dump confirms this — every F32 entry in the dump is a norm weight (small, cheap), every Q4_K/Q6_K is a projection weight.
